A Comparative Analysis of Machine Learning Methods for Genomic Variants Classification

Paper Submission: This research has been submitted to the IEEE Engineering in Medicine and Biology Conference (EMBC). The paper will be published upon acceptance.

About This Research

This research project addresses a critical bottleneck in clinical genomics: the accurate classification of genomic variants to determine their clinical significance (benign, pathogenic, or uncertain significance).

Conducted at NECSTLab, Politecnico di Milano, this work was developed in collaboration with Samuele Tondelli (equal contribution) and under the supervision of Leonardo De Grandis. The research was also conducted in partnership with GenoGra srl.

The interpretation of genomic variants remains one of the most challenging aspects of clinical genomics. While next-generation sequencing technologies can detect millions of variants, a substantial proportion are labeled as variants of uncertain significance (VUS), creating diagnostic uncertainty. Current manual interpretation approaches, guided by ACMG/AMP guidelines, are labor-intensive, subjective, and inconsistent across different clinical laboratories.

This study systematically evaluates multiple machine learning approaches to provide an evidence-based roadmap for developing computationally efficient and clinically robust variant classification models, optimizing the balance between resource requirements and diagnostic performance.

Methodology

We conducted a comprehensive comparative analysis, systematically evaluating 4 machine learning models across 144 different configurations. The study spanned:

  • Multiple dataset sizes to assess the impact of training data volume
  • Three feature selection strategies to evaluate feature importance and redundancy
  • Four classification tasks including binary and multi-class scenarios
  • dbNSFP v5.0a dataset with ClinVar annotations as the primary data source

The models evaluated included tree-based classifiers (Random Forest, XGBoost), logistic regression, and neural networks. The experimental pipeline was built using Snakemake for workflow management and Hydra for configuration management, ensuring reproducibility and scalability of the experiments.

Key Findings

Our comprehensive analysis revealed several critical insights for resource-efficient variant classification:

  • XGBoost consistently achieved the best performance across all evaluated configurations
  • All models reached F1 scores exceeding 0.96 for binary classification tasks, demonstrating clinically competitive performance
  • Training set size threshold: Increasing the training set beyond 100,000 samples yields only marginal performance gains across all models, indicating an optimal data efficiency point
  • Feature selection impact: Feature selection exerts a more substantial impact on performance than dataset size, especially for multi-class classification tasks
  • VUS classification: Models trained exclusively on high-confidence benign and pathogenic variants showed high concordance when applied to uncertain variants, despite substantial differences in architecture
  • Feature importance: SHAP analysis revealed consistent patterns across models, with functional prediction scores and rank scores consistently ranking highest in importance

Top 7 Features by Importance

Functional prediction scores and rank scores consistently rank highest in importance across all models.

Clinical Impact & Future Directions

These findings establish practical guidelines for resource-efficient variant classification, demonstrating that clinically competitive performance can be achieved with substantially reduced computational requirements. This is particularly important for clinical deployment, where computational resources and training time are often constrained.

The study also highlights important areas for future research, including addressing population representation imbalances in genomic databases and exploring more sophisticated feature selection. Additionally, the lower ranking stability observed in neural network models compared to logistic regression suggests opportunities for architecture-specific feature engineering approaches. A promising direction could involve leveraging Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to exploit graph structures that connect genomic elements and preserve rich relational information, potentially capturing complex biological relationships that traditional feature-based approaches might miss.
